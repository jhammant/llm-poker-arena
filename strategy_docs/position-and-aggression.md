# Position, initiative and aggression

A practical primer for heads-up No-Limit Hold'em at ~100bb effective stacks. In heads-up, the small blind is the button (SB/BTN) and acts last on every postflop street; the big blind (BB) acts first postflop. Read this as a decision aid: prefer the in-position, aggressive line unless a specific reason says otherwise.

## Why acting last wins

Position is information. When you act last you see your opponent's action before you commit, so every decision is made with strictly more data. That single fact lets you:

- Realize your equity more cheaply — check back marginal hands for a free card instead of facing a bet.
- Control pot size — make it big with strong hands, small with mediocre ones.
- Bluff with better information, since you saw weakness (a check) first.

Concretely: a 9-high flush draw out of position must guess; in position you can take the free card when checked to, or bet/fold when raised. Same cards, more EV. Treat being in position as worth roughly an extra 2-4bb of value per orbit versus the same range out of position — enough to flip many breakeven spots into profit.

## The value of initiative

The aggressor wins pots two ways: opponent folds (fold equity) or opponent calls and you have the best hand (showdown equity). A bet earns both; a check earns only the second. This is why aggression dominates passive play — you collect pots that checking down would surrender.

Initiative is the right to keep representing strength. When you raised preflop and bet the flop, you stay "the story-teller": your line is consistent with the strongest hands, so your opponent is the one forced to make tough decisions. Passing initiative (checking, flat-calling) hands that authority to them.

Math intuition: if you bet half pot, opponent must fold more than 33% of the time for the bluff to be immediately profitable (risk 0.5 to win 1.0 ⇒ 0.5/1.5 = 33%). Bet two-thirds pot, they must fold 40%. So even hopeless hands print money when the bet is sized so the required fold frequency is plausible.

## Continuation betting

You raised preflop, so you should usually keep betting the flop. As the SB/BTN raiser in position, c-bet around 60-75% of flops. The bet does triple duty: denies equity to overcards/draws, builds the pot with your value, and keeps your range uncapped.

Sizing:

- Dry, static boards (e.g. K72 rainbow, A83 rainbow): small, 25-33% pot, at high frequency (~80%+). Cheap, hard to attack, applies to your whole range.
- Wet, dynamic boards (e.g. JT8 two-tone, 976 two-tone): larger, 60-75% pot, but more selectively. Charge draws and pick spots; check back some marginal hands.

C-bet less when the board smashes the caller's range more than yours (low connected boards out of position often favor the BB), and on those boards lean toward checking or small sizing.

## Barreling (turn and river)

A flop c-bet that gets called is the start of a story, not the end. Pick up the barrel on:

- Turns that improve your perceived range — overcards to the flop, your draws completing, scare cards (an A or K rolling off).
- Boards where the caller's flop-calling range is mostly weak pairs and busted draws.

Good double-barrel frequency after a flop c-bet is roughly 45-60%. Size up turns to 66-75% pot to maximize fold equity and set up an all-in shove on the river. The third barrel is the scariest bet in poker — reserve big rivers for genuine value plus a disciplined slice of bluffs (your missed draws), staying near a 2:1 value-to-bluff ratio on large bets.

## Why passive / station play loses

Calling without a plan is the losing default. A calling station pays off every value bet and never collects fold equity, so they win small and lose big — the worst possible variance shape. Flat-calling preflop out of position invites the aggressor to barrel you off the best hand. Limping the button surrenders initiative and range advantage for nothing. If a hand is good enough to continue, it is usually good enough to raise or bet; if it is not, fold. "Call to see what happens" is how chips leak.

## How heads-up amplifies position

Heads-up there is no one to your left to wake up — you face exactly one range, and you are in position half the time by force (you are always SB/BTN or BB). Ranges are far wider than full-ring, so flops connect with neither player most of the time; whoever bets usually wins. That hands enormous edge to the aggressor with position. Raise your button relentlessly (open ~80-100% of buttons), 3-bet liberally to seize initiative, and lean on c-bets because air-vs-air is the most common matchup and the bettor takes it.

## Quick rules

- In position, bet for value and bluff freely; out of position, simplify and fold more.
- When unsure between checking and betting in position, bet — initiative pays twice.
- C-bet small and often on dry boards (25-33% pot), big and selective on wet boards (60-75%).
- Half-pot needs 33% folds, two-thirds-pot needs 40% — size bluffs to plausible fold rates.
- Barrel turns that scare the caller's range; give up when it improves theirs.
- Never limp the button and never flat out of position with a hand worth raising.
- Stations lose: stop calling to "see what happens" — raise, bet, or fold.
- Open ~90% of buttons; wide ranges mean the aggressor with position wins the pot nobody hit.
