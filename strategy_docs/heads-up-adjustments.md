# Heads-up specific adjustments and exploits

Heads-up (HU) No-Limit Hold'em is a different game from full-ring. There are only two players, you post a blind every single hand, and the small blind is the button (acts first preflop, last postflop). Every hand is blind-vs-blind. The default mindset: chips left in your stack do nothing — chips you win do everything. Folding marginal spots bleeds you to death faster than any single bad call.

## Blind-vs-blind dynamics

You pay a blind every hand, so passing hands away is expensive. Assume ~100bb effective, SB = button.

- As SB/button you have position for the whole hand and act last postflop. This is huge. Open-raise a very wide range: roughly the top 80-90% of hands. Default open size 2-2.5bb (or limp/raise mix vs specific opponents). Folding the button is almost always a leak — even 7-2o open-folds only when the BB punishes openers hard.
- As BB you are getting a great price to defend (you've already invested 1bb). Versus a min-raise you should continue with ~60-70% of hands — call wide, 3-bet your strong and some bluff hands (3-bet to ~3.5x the open). Over-folding the BB hands free chips to the button.

## Why you must play wide and aggressive

Hand values rise dramatically two-handed. Any ace, any king, any pair, any two Broadway cards, suited connectors and most suited gappers are playable or raisable. A hand like K6o is a clear button open; J9o has real equity; bottom pair frequently wins at showdown. Because ranges are wide, top pair is a monster and even ace-high or middle pair has showdown value worth protecting.

Aggression compounds. With wide ranges, most boards miss most hands, so the player who bets first and most often wins the most uncontested pots. Continuation-bet flops frequently (~50-70%) using small sizing (~33% pot) on dry boards and larger (~66-75%) on wet, draw-heavy boards. Apply turn and river pressure when the opponent shows weakness. The goal is to make the opponent make decisions for stacks repeatedly — that is where mistakes (and your profit) come from.

## Exploiting a passive opponent (bet relentlessly)

A passive opponent checks when weak and only bets/raises when strong. Counter:

- Bet for value AND as a bluff at high frequency. If they don't raise, your bluffs print money. Fire flop, turn, and often river with any equity or any blockers.
- When they finally do raise or lead big, believe them — fold your weak made hands and bluffs to aggression, because their aggression is uncapped honesty.
- Thin-value bet relentlessly: second pair, ace-high, even king-high can value bet the river against a station who calls too wide, while a passive-but-tight player should be value-bet thinner and bluffed more.

## Exploiting an aggressive opponent (trap and call down)

A maniac bets and raises too much. You do not need to out-bluff them — let them bluff into you.

- Widen your calling/check-call ranges substantially. Call down with any pair, strong ace-high, and good draws. Their range is full of air, so your bluff-catchers gain value.
- Trap your strong hands: check-call or slow-play sets, two pair, and top pair to keep their barrels coming rather than blowing them off their bluffs.
- Re-bluff less; instead, let them hang themselves, then raise for value on later streets when you're strong. Reduce your own c-bet bluffs since they won't fold anyway.

## Calling station vs. nit

- Calling station (calls too much, folds too little): stop bluffing entirely — bluffs are lit on fire. Value bet relentlessly and thinly; size up your value bets (75-100%+ pot) because they pay regardless. Every made hand of medium strength becomes a value bet.
- Nit (folds too much, only continues with premiums): bluff relentlessly. Open every button, c-bet nearly always, barrel multiple streets. Steal blinds at will. When the nit finally puts in real money, fold everything but your strongest holdings — give them no action and take the rest with relentless small aggression.

## The cost of folding too much

Folding too much is the single biggest HU leak. Each fold as BB surrenders 1bb you already posted; each fold of the button surrenders the chance to win a pot you would usually win. An opponent who notices you over-fold can profitably open and c-bet any two cards, taxing you blind after blind. Defend enough to make their bluffs break even, then over-defend slightly against anyone who bets too often. Calling a river bluff-catcher only needs to be right about as often as the pot odds imply — fold a touch less than feels comfortable. Tie everything back to the only goal: accumulate chips by forcing the opponent into more, and worse, decisions than you make.

## Quick rules

- Open the button almost always; min-raise wide, fold the button rarely.
- Defend the big blind ~60-70%; punish small opens by over-defending.
- Bet relentlessly vs. passive players; trap and call down vs. aggressive ones.
- Never bluff a calling station; never stop bluffing a nit.
- Top pair and even ace-high are strong two-handed — protect and value them.
- Folding too much is your costliest leak; call one notch wider than comfortable.
- Make every pot a decision for your opponent; profit lives in their mistakes.
